Packet Sniffing/Spoofing Project

The goal of this project is to create a packet sniffing tool that is capable of filtering for packets of a specific type, from a specific source, or sent to a specific destination. This tool is both modular and simple. By the end of the project the tool was capable of sniffing for packets of a specific type and then checking if that packet is an echo-request type packet. If the packet was, then the tool took the destination address of said packet, used that address as the source, and then sent a spoofed echo-reply to that request. This tool was made on the linux platform using Python and Scapy and was tested by using two separate virtual machines. The below picture is a snippet of the tool by the end of the project. The full documentation of the lab is linked below. This document has more in depth pictures and a step by step walkthrough of what I did.

Certificate Authority & PKI

The goal of this project was to create a certificate authority (CA), implant that CA in the firefox browser, and then use that CA to establish a secure SSL connection with a web server. This process involved first creating the self signed root certificate for the CA. This root certificate is then used to sign signing requests made by webservers that wish to establish secure connections. The webserver must generate a key pair, and then send a signing request that includes their public key. The details of this lab and the step by step process are outlined in detail within the document attached. Mobile User Experience

Study Goal: The goal of this study is to determine what aspects of mobile shopping would attract more users from the ages of 18-25. Mobile (app) shopping doesn’t have as much traction as desktop shopping so the goal is to determine what aspects users like about both and what aspects would draw more user towards a mobile shopping approach. Typically, users like a convenient experience so I will try and determine what factors would make mobile shopping convenient.

Target Audience: The target audience is college students. This audience is most likely to embrace shopping through apps because they understand technology better than older generations. They also have more financial independence than younger generations. This puts them in the ideal position to transition from desktop based online shopping to a more convenient mobile option. College students are also very mobile throughout the day so a more convenient shopping option would be more easily adopted.

Research Methods: For this project I conducted interviews to gather information about mobile shopping design. Interviews allowed me to directly interact with users of these applications and determine aspects people liked and disliked.

Recruitment: I recruited my users by asking piers if they would like to participate in a short interview. They then went through the online survey to determine if they fit the criteria.

Number of Participants: 3

Key Insights: Applications save information, Desktop is more usable overall, UI isn't optimal on mobile.

Park-It Group Project

Key Responsibilities: My responsibilities for this project were to work with the team to develop a strong study goal but mostly I worked on the prototype. I was responsible for the design of the prototype, the creation of it, and the implementation of the functionalities to the prototype. Once we were given feedback about our initial prototype I was responsible for making updates to the prototype to reflect these changes. For the design of the prototype I used AdobeXD and for the implementation of the functionality I used Invision.

Study Goal: Searching for parking is nuisance for many people who live in urban areas, commute, or simply enjoy attending large events. While there are often sections that are numbered and lettered in large areas, many people forget where they park their car, while others have a very hard time even searching for a spot; this causes an enormous waste of time for many people. While there are apps that provide users with parking prices, and available garages, there are none to our knowledge that provides specific details. Details such as exact location of parking spot, availability of spaces, and even more detailed information about where an available spot is located. Team 6 plans on exploring how we can provide a mobile application that will help with this problem.

Target Audience: The community we’ve decided to focus on is for this specific project is drivers in State College, specifically around the Penn State campus. while this may change in the future for expansion. However, we have chosen Penn State due to the large volume of people that attend the many sporting events, concerts, enjoy tailgating, and other events that take place on campus.

Research Methods: For this project my group and I conducted multiple interviews with participants that fit the outlined target audience. From these interviews we extracted a lot of information about what causes people annoyances when trying to park and some key insights into possible solutions. Another large research method we used was observation. Each member of the group was responsible for observing people try and find parking spots in different parking lots. I personally watched the student Lot 43 for people trying to park and observed the difficulties people would face when looking for a spot.

First App Design: Below is the first design for the app before we redesigned it using feedback. The actual prototype was edited so we only have screenshots of the original design to show.

Parkin is most challenging, stressful, and expensive during event weekends. Tailgating fields do not get good signal making it difficult to find spaces. Searching for tailgating spots is a long exasperating process. Drivers need to arrive very early for good parking. Parking garages are often full on the weekends. Student lots and apartment lots are often full during the weekdays. There are many restrictions on lots around campus. Student parking is very expensive and the lots are far from classes and the students’ residences.

Cross-Site Request Forgery

In this lab, my group and I executed a simple Cross-Site Request forgery attack on a simulated social media site. It outlined how simple of an attack this can be, and how vulnerable people can be to this attack without having the proper defenses in place. This attack involved taking advantage of the cookie used to maintain a loged-in connection, and then forcing the target to click a malicious link that forced an action on their account. The first step of this attack was to analyse the POST and GET requests made on the site when adding friends or updating an account legitimately. This legitimate network traffic was used to craft malicious code that would launch a request that forcibly added a user to the targets friends list, and even updating the targets account with information. The steps taken during this process as well as detailed explanations of this process are outlined in the document linked below. The picutre below shows a POST and GET request being analyzed.
